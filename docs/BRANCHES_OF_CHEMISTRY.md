# Branches of Chemistry

> Chemistry has several distinct branches that approach matter from very different angles. The boundaries between them are useful for organizing knowledge but artificial in practice — the most interesting work almost always happens at intersections.

---

## Core Branches

**Organic Chemistry**
The study of carbon-based molecules. Covers essentially everything alive and most synthetic materials — drugs, plastics, fuels, food. The largest branch by volume of research. Notorious for being one of the hardest undergraduate courses because of the sheer number of reactions and mechanisms to memorize.

**Inorganic Chemistry**
Everything that isn't organic — metals, minerals, salts, ceramics, semiconductors. Studies how non-carbon elements bond and behave. Overlaps heavily with materials science and geology.

**Physical Chemistry**
Where chemistry meets physics. Studies the underlying principles — thermodynamics, quantum mechanics, reaction rates, molecular structure. Less about specific substances and more about the rules governing all of them. The most mathematical branch.

**Analytical Chemistry**
The branch focused on identifying and measuring what something is made of. Every time a crime lab tests a substance, a doctor runs bloodwork, or an environmental agency checks water quality — that's analytical chemistry. Develops the tools and techniques the other branches rely on.

**Biochemistry**
The chemistry of living systems. Proteins, DNA, metabolism, enzymes, hormones. Sits at the intersection of chemistry and biology. Probably the fastest-growing branch given where medicine and biotechnology are heading.

**Computational Chemistry**
Uses computers and mathematical models to simulate molecular behavior, predict reactions, and explore chemical space without physically synthesizing anything. Increasingly important for drug discovery — you can screen millions of potential molecules virtually before making a single one in a lab.

## Specialized Branches

**Nuclear Chemistry**
Reactions involving atomic nuclei — radioactive decay, fission, fusion. More physics-adjacent than typical chemistry. Directly connected to the Nucleus class in the game.

**Electrochemistry**
The relationship between electricity and chemical reactions. Batteries, fuel cells, corrosion, and electroplating all live here. Connected to the electron-as-mana system at the core of the game's resource mechanic.

**Polymer Chemistry**
Specifically focused on large chain molecules. Plastics, rubber, textiles, biological polymers. Directly connected to the monomer/polymer concepts in MOLECULAR_STRUCTURE.md.

**Medicinal Chemistry**
Designing and synthesizing drug molecules. Sits at the crossroads of organic chemistry, biochemistry, and pharmacology. Where most modern pharmaceuticals come from.

**Geochemistry**
The chemistry of the Earth itself — how elements distribute through rocks, oceans, and the atmosphere over geological time. Connected to where elements are found (the whereFound field in the database).

**Astrochemistry**
The chemistry of space. What molecules exist in nebulae, on other planets, in comets. How molecules form in the extreme conditions of the universe. Connected to the origin stories of elements forged in stars — the lore backbone of the Forge and Nucleus classes.

**Green Chemistry**
A philosophy as much as a branch. Designing chemical processes that minimize waste, toxicity, and environmental impact. Less a subject of study and more an ethical framework applied across all other branches.

## Intersections Worth Noting

- **Biochemistry** = biology + chemistry
- **Medicinal chemistry** = organic + biochemistry + pharmacology
- **Computational chemistry** = physical chemistry + computer science
- **Geochemistry** = inorganic + geology + physics
- **Astrochemistry** = physical + nuclear + inorganic, in extreme conditions

The game already touches several of these without naming them: electrochemistry (electron resource system), nuclear chemistry (Nucleus class, decay mechanic), polymer chemistry (chain-building concepts), and astrochemistry (stellar origin lore for heavy elements).
