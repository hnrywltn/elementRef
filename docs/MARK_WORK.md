# Mark's Work — Character Development

## Context

Mark is Henry's younger brother, in his 30s, on the autistic spectrum. He is a savant when it comes to film and television — he can recite full episodes of a multitude of shows verbatim, knows actors, characters, directors, producers, and is a particular expert in horror. He has already been contributing to the plushie side of the project by helping develop personality descriptors for each element character.

This document captures the plan to bring him into the card game side of the project specifically around **character personality development** for all 118 element cards.

---

## The Idea

Every element card needs a personality — a voice, a way of moving through the world, quirks, motivations, a feel. Writing 118 original personalities from scratch is a large and abstract task. 

Mark's knowledge offers a shortcut that is also more precise than writing from scratch: **use existing TV and film characters as personality anchors.**

If an element's personality is "like Walter White in season 3" — that communicates instantly. The designer knows the paranoia, the pride, the calculated coldness, the slow corruption. It takes seconds to convey what paragraphs might struggle to capture. And because Mark's library spans thousands of shows and characters — including obscure ones most people have forgotten — the references won't all default to the same ten famous archetypes. The obscure references are often more interesting and more specific.

This is an internal creative tool. The character references don't necessarily appear in the game — they're a shared language for the design team to align on personality before writing actual flavor text, lore, and ability descriptions.

---

## Why This Works Well for This Project

**Horror maps naturally onto the element categories.** Mark's specialty — horror — overlaps heavily with the most interesting element classes. Radioactive elements, toxic elements, the Nucleus and Venom classes are full of horror-adjacent qualities: power that corrupts, transformation, decay, things that seem stable until they suddenly aren't. His deepest knowledge is directly relevant to some of the most important cards.

**Breadth matters as much as depth.** 118 elements is a lot of personalities. Having access to someone who knows thousands of characters across decades of television and film means the reference pool is large enough to find something specific for every element — not just the obvious ones.

**Systematic recall is a design asset.** Mark's memory is likely more systematic and less filtered by popular opinion than most people's. He probably has strong takes on characters others have forgotten or overlooked, which is exactly what makes the references interesting. The goal is specificity, not familiarity.

---

## How It Works in Practice

A dedicated page in the admin panel — **Character Development** — shows all 118 elements as a to-do list. For each element, Mark (or anyone contributing) can log:

- The character name
- The show or movie
- The actor who plays them
- Any relevant notes (specific season, specific arc, what specifically maps to this element)

Multiple references per element are allowed — sometimes one character captures the personality exactly, sometimes it takes two or three references to triangulate.

The page gives a clear view of progress across all 118 elements: which ones have references, which ones are still blank.

---

## Connection to Game Design

Once character references exist for an element, they become the source material for:

- `characterName` — the element's in-game persona name
- `loreDescription` — the character's story and personality in the game world
- `abilityDescription` — abilities can be shaped by personality (a paranoid character might have a counter-ability; a charming one might have a debuff that works through distraction)
- Artwork direction — the visual design of the card can draw from the character reference

The character reference is the creative seed. Everything else grows from it.

## Open Questions

- Does each element get one definitive reference, or is it a collection that gets narrowed down?
- Are horror characters weighted toward specific element classes (Nucleus, Venom) or distributed freely based on personality fit?
- Does Mark work through the elements in periodic table order, by class, or by whatever he finds interesting first?
- How do we handle elements where the personality is subtle or scientific rather than dramatic — the noble gases, the structural metals?
